# Quotient Tests and Gröbner Bases

## Abstract

Given a finitely presented group G = ⟨X; R⟩, the word problem asks to decide effectively whether a given word in the free group w ∈ F(X) represents the identity element in G or not. Another important computational problem is to check effectively whether G is finite or not. For the "No" parts of these problems, we can use quotient tests. For instance, for the "No" part of the word problem, we can devise a procedure which proves w ≠_G 1 or returns "Don't know" if we have an effectively computable group homomorphism φ: G → H and if the "No" part of the word problem is decidable in H. To find a suitable group homomorphism φ: G → H, we use universal linear representations of G. Here the universal linear representation ϱ: G → SL(n, Q_R) is constructed as follows. We map each generator x_k of G to a matrix of indeterminates. In the polynomial ring P having these indeterminates, we form the ideal I_R generated by the entries of the images of the relators in R and by the polynomials det(a^(k)_{i,j}) − 1. Letting Q_R = R/I_R, we obtain a well-defined linear representation ϱ: G → SL(n, Q_R). At this point Gröbner bases come into play: if we succeed in computing a Gröbner basis of the ideal I_R, we can check effectively whether the matrix of polynomials ϱ(w) defines the identity element in ϱ(G). Moreover, we have several methods available for showing that ϱ(G), and hence G, is an infinite group. We apply these techniques to Kourovka Notebook problems, groups of deficiency zero, and the classification of finite generalized triangle and tetrahedron groups.

## TL;DR

This is the foundational paper establishing the universal linear representation approach to the word problem and infinitude detection for finitely presented groups. It introduces Algorithm 5.1 (universal linear quotient test, a "No"-only partial WP oracle via Gröbner basis reduction in SL(n, Q_R)) and Algorithm 6.2 (minimal polynomial test for infinitude). Concrete applications: Kourovka problem groups G_i and H_i, deficiency-zero groups, and 5 open generalized tetrahedron groups. The 2025 draft [[grobner]] is its direct descendant.

## Problem

Two fundamental computational problems for finitely presented groups G = ⟨X; R⟩:
1. **Word problem ("No" direction)**: Given w ∈ F(X), certify w ≠_G 1. (The full WP is undecidable in general; only the "No" part is targeted.)
2. **Infinitude detection**: Certify that G is an infinite group.

Both are addressed using group homomorphisms to groups with decidable word problem — specifically, to SL(n, K) for tractable rings K derived from the relators.

## Approach

**Step 1 — Universal linear representation (§4):**

For G = ⟨x₁,...,x_s; r₁,...,r_t⟩, introduce indeterminates T = {y^(k)_{ij} | k=1..s, i,j=1..n}. Define the ideal I_R ⊂ ℤ[T] generated by:
- det(x^ϱ_k) − 1 = 0 for each generator x_k (det-one condition)
- (ϱ(r))_{i,j} − δ_{i,j} = 0 for each relator r ∈ R (relator equations, system S_R)

Universal representation ring: Q_R = ℤ[T]/I_R. Map ϱ: G → SL(n, Q_R) by x_k ↦ (y^(k)_{ij}). By Proposition 4.2, every SL(n,K)-representation of G factors through ϱ via a ring map Q_R → K.

**Optimization (Remark 3.1):** Split each relator r = r′ ∘ r″ and encode as ϱ(r′) = ϱ((r″)^{-1}). This halves the polynomial degree — splitting in the middle minimizes degree.

**Step 2 — Algorithm 5.1 (Universal Linear Quotient Test, §5):**
1. Compute S_R (polynomial equations from relators per (3)).
2. Compute Gröbner basis for I_R in ℚ[T].
3. Compute ϱ(w) = ϱ(x_{i₁})⋯ϱ(x_{i_ℓ}) symbolically (matrix product of generator images).
4. Let ϱ(w) = (f_{i,j}). Reduce each f_{i,j} − δ_{i,j} modulo the Gröbner basis. If any f_{i,j} − δ_{i,j} ∉ I_R: **return "No"**. If all zero: **return "Don't know."**

**Step 3 — Algorithm 6.2 (Minimal Polynomial of a Matrix, §6):**

Input: W = (w_{i,j}) ∈ Mat(n, K) where K = F[y₁,...,yₘ]/I is an affine algebra over a field F.
1. In F[y₁,...,yₘ, z]^n, form the submodule U generated by columns of W − z·I_n and elements of I·F[y₁,...,yₘ,z]^n.
2. Compute the ideal V = U : ⟨e₁,...,e_n⟩ in F[y₁,...,yₘ,z].
3. By elimination, compute V ∩ F[z] and return a monic generator (the minimal polynomial).
If the minimal polynomial is 0, or does not divide z^m − 1 for any m, the element has infinite order → G is infinite.

**Step 4 — Algorithm 6.4 (Diagonal Quotient Test for Infinitude, §6):**

Simpler heuristic: for each generator x_k, check whether the diagonal entries (y^(k)_{ℓ,ℓ}) appear with bounded leading terms in the Gröbner basis of I_R. If no leading term of the form (y^(k)_{ℓ,ℓ})^N exists, then x_k generates an infinite cyclic group → G is infinite.

## Key result

**Theorem / Proposition 4.2** (§4): Let G = ⟨X; R⟩ be a finitely presented group, and let Q_R = ℤ[T]/I_R be constructed as above. Then the group homomorphism ϱ: G → SL(n, Q_R) defined by x_k ↦ (y^(k)_{(1,1)},...,y^(k)_{(n,n)}) is a universal linear representation of G. (Every SL(n,L)-representation of G factors through ϱ via a ring map Q_R → L.)

**Proposition 8.3** (§8): Let K be a commutative unitary ring, u,v ∈ SL(2,K)\{1,−1}.
1. If tr(u) = 0 then u² = −1.
2. If u² = −1 then (tr(u))² = 0.
3. If u² = 1 then (tr(u))² = 4.
4. For all k ≥ 1 and αᵢ,βⱼ ∈ ℕ: tr(u^{α₁}v^{β₁}⋯u^{αₖ}v^{βₖ}) = tr(u^{−α₁}v^{−β₁}⋯u^{−αₖ}v^{−βₖ}).

**Worked applications (§7):**

- **Kourovka Problem 7.7/8.10 — groups G_i = ⟨a,b; a^i=1, ab=b³a³⟩**: Using n=2 and a truncated Gröbner basis, shown that φ₂(G_i) is commutative for i=5,7,9,11, hence these representation quotients are finite of order 2i. (Note: G₉ and G₁₅ are known to be infinite; the result is for the n=2 *image*, not G itself.)
- **Kourovka Problem 11.10b — groups H_i = ⟨a,b; a^i=1, ababa=b²ab⁻¹ab⟩**: By computing complete Gröbner bases at n=2, proved a ≠ 1 in H_i for i=5,10,15,20. For all other H_i with i ≤ 20 (including i=2,3,4,6,...), x = φ₂(a) = 1 in the n=2 representation.
- **Deficiency-zero groups F₁₁, F₁₂, F₁₃ (§7.3)**: Reduced DegRevLex Gröbner basis of I_R for φ₂ has 117 elements; φ₂(F₁₁) ≅ φ₂(F₁₂) ≅ φ₂(F₁₃) ≅ ℤ/(3) × ℤ/(3) × ℤ/(3). (F₁₁ finiteness unknown; F₁₃ later shown infinite by Holt via different methods.)
- **Generalized tetrahedron groups T₁–T₅ (§8)**: All five 2×2 representations are finite; infinitude of the groups themselves remains open. Proposition 8.3 is the key structural lemma.

## Assumptions

- Gröbner basis computation for I_R terminates (not guaranteed — doubly exponential worst case in the number of indeterminates; tractable only for small n and short relators).
- The polynomial encoding correctly captures the group structure (follows from the universal property of Q_R, Proposition 4.2).
- The split-relator optimization (Remark 3.1) is applied: each relator r ∈ R is divided at its midpoint to halve polynomial degree.
- For Algorithm 6.2: the affine algebra K = F[y₁,...,yₘ]/I is finitely generated over a field F.

## Limitations / scope

- The word-problem test (Algorithm 5.1) is **semi-decidable**: certifies "No" (w ≠ 1) but never "Yes" (w = 1). A "Don't know" outcome is uninformative for groups of unknown finiteness.
- **No treatment of B(2,5) or any Burnside group** — unlike the 2025 descendant [[grobner]], this paper's examples are confined to Kourovka problems 7.7/8.10/11.10b, deficiency-zero groups, and generalized triangle/tetrahedron groups.
- For n=3, Gröbner bases are described as "difficult to compute at present" or only tractable in truncated form — the n=2 restriction applies to most results.
- Commutativity of φ₂(G_i) does not imply commutativity of G_i itself; the result concerns the representation image only (indeed G₉ is known infinite).

## Replication evidence

Partial. The Kourovka H_i results (a ≠ 1 in H₅,H₁₀,H₁₅,H₂₀) agree with the known result a ≠ 1 in H₅ from the Kourovka Notebook itself. The deficiency-zero group computations (F₁₁, F₁₂, F₁₃ having n=2 image ≅ ℤ/(3)³) and the tetrahedron group results are computational — no independent replication identified in this vault. M.F. Newman confirmed F₁₃ infinite by independent methods (cited as personal communication, 2005).

## Why this paper matters

This is the originating paper for the Gröbner-basis approach to word problems in finitely presented groups via universal linear representations. It introduces the three core constructions — the representation ring Q_R, the quotient test (Algorithm 5.1), and the infinitude probe (Algorithm 6.2) — that the 2025 descendant [[grobner]] refines and applies to harder problems including B(2,5).

The three concept hubs [[Concepts/grobner-quotient-filter]], [[Concepts/grobner-infinitude-probe]], and [[Concepts/representation-variety]] describe machinery first introduced here. Understanding this paper clarifies why those concepts work: the universal property (Proposition 4.2) is the theoretical guarantor that working with Q_R covers all SL(n,K)-representations at once.

The paper's honesty about computational limits — truncated Gröbner bases for n=3, failure of 2×2 representations to resolve tetrahedron group infinitude — sets the practical scope that the 2025 work inherits.

## Open questions surfaced

- For which finitely presented groups does the truncated Gröbner basis at n=2 suffice to resolve word problem / infinitude questions? The deficiency-zero groups and tetrahedron groups at n=3 are noted as computationally intractable — what are the precise size thresholds?
- G₇ = ⟨a,b; a⁷=1, ab=b³a³⟩: is it finite or infinite? (Open as of this paper; the n=2 quotient is commutative-finite but G₇ may still be infinite.)
- The five generalized tetrahedron groups T₁–T₅: all 2×2 representations are finite; can the groups themselves be shown infinite or finite? The 3×3 computations are currently intractable.
- How far can B(2,5) be approached via this method? (The 2025 descendant [[grobner]] attempts this in §7.5 but leaves it incomplete.)
- Can the split-relator optimization (Remark 3.1) be pushed further — e.g., splitting relators into thirds or more?
- The "diagonal quotient test" (Algorithm 6.4) is noted as "usually weak" — for which group families does it actually succeed?
