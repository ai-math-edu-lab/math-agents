# Deep Network Guided Proof Search

## Abstract

"Deep learning techniques lie at the heart of several significant AI advances in recent years including object recognition and detection, image captioning, machine translation, speech recognition and synthesis, and playing the game of Go. Automated first-order theorem provers can aid in the formalization and verification of mathematical theorems and play a crucial role in program analysis, theory reasoning, security, interpolation, and system verification. Here we suggest deep learning based guidance in the proof search of the theorem prover E. We train and compare several deep neural network models on the traces of existing ATP proofs of Mizar statements and use them to select processed clauses during proof search. We give experimental evidence that with a hybrid, two-phase approach, deep learning based guidance can significantly reduce the average number of proof search steps while increasing the number of theorems proved. Using a few proof guidance strategies that leverage deep neural networks, we have found first-order proofs of 7.36% of the first-order logic translations of the Mizar Mathematical Library theorems that did not previously have ATP generated proofs. This increases the ratio of statements in the corpus with ATP generated proofs from 56% to 59%."

## TL;DR

First use of deep neural networks to guide proof search inside an existing ATP prover (E theorem prover): DNNs trained on Mizar proof traces select which clauses to process during proof search, replacing handcrafted heuristics. Found proofs for 7.36% of previously-unproved Mizar theorems. Foundational "ML inside existing solver" paper.

## Problem

ATP provers like E use heuristics (e.g., weight/symbol-counting) to select which clauses to process during proof search. These heuristics are hand-designed and don't adapt to the mathematical domain. Can deep neural networks, trained on successful proof traces, replace or augment these heuristics?

## Approach

**Two-phase hybrid approach:**
1. **Neural guidance**: a DNN trained on Mizar proof traces selects promising clauses for the E prover to process. The DNN takes a representation of the current clause (and proof state context) and scores its likelihood of being useful in a proof.
2. **Fallback**: if neural guidance fails, the prover falls back to standard heuristics.

**Training data**: traces of successful E proofs on Mizar Mathematical Library translations to first-order logic. The DNN learns to recognize "proof-relevant" clauses from these traces.

**Multiple DNN models compared**: linear, shallow neural network, deep network — results show deeper networks improve performance.

## Key result

- **7.36% of previously-unproved Mizar theorems** now have proofs — the absolute new-proof rate.
- **Corpus coverage**: 56% → 59% of Mizar corpus now has ATP-generated proofs.
- **Proof step reduction**: "significantly reduces the average number of proof search steps" with neural guidance.

## Assumptions

- Mizar Mathematical Library (100k+ theorems in first-order logic) is a representative mathematical corpus for ATP evaluation.
- Neural networks trained on successful proof traces generalize to unseen theorems from the same corpus.
- E prover's clause selection is the main bottleneck for improving coverage.

## Limitations / scope

- E theorem prover only (first-order logic, resolution-based); not applicable to type-theoretic proof assistants (Lean 4, Isabelle).
- Mizar corpus only; generalization to other formal libraries not tested.
- Neural guidance is used for clause selection only — not for higher-level proof planning or auxiliary construction.

## Replication evidence

The approach is extended in [[1805.07563]] (Kaliszyk et al. 2018, NeurIPS) which replaces the neural heuristic with RL-trained MCTS, achieving much larger gains. The core "neural guidance inside existing ATP" paradigm is validated by [[1805.07563]].

## Why this paper matters

Loos et al. 2017 is the foundational paper for "neural networks as heuristics inside existing ATP solvers" — the ML-for-math paradigm that relies on existing symbolic reasoning engines but improves their guidance. This is distinct from the LLM-prover paradigm (which replaces the symbolic engine) and from the neuro-symbolic paradigm (which combines neural and symbolic in a tighter loop).

The same paradigm appears in [[2306.15626]] (LeanDojo/ReProver) — premise selection via neural retrieval inside a proof assistant. Both papers use ML to answer "which existing facts should I consider?" rather than "generate a proof from scratch."

Note on authorship: Kaliszyk is a co-author here and the first author of [[1805.07563]] (RL for theorem proving) — this represents a continuous research program from neural guidance (2017) to RL-based MCTS (2018).
