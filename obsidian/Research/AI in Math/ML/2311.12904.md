# Learning to Compute Gröbner Bases

## Abstract

Solving a polynomial system, or computing an associated Gröbner basis, has been a fundamental task in computational algebra. However, it is also known for its notorious doubly exponential time complexity in the number of variables in the worst case. This paper is the first to address the learning of Gröbner basis computation with Transformers. The training requires many pairs of a polynomial system and the associated Gröbner basis, raising two novel algebraic problems: random generation of Gröbner bases and transforming them into non-Gröbner ones, termed as backward Gröbner problem. We resolve these problems with 0-dimensional radical ideals, the ideals appearing in various applications. Further, we propose a hybrid input embedding to handle coefficient tokens with continuity bias and avoid the growth of the vocabulary set. The experiments show that our dataset generation method is a few orders of magnitude faster than a naive approach, overcoming a crucial challenge in learning to compute Gröbner bases, and Gröbner computation is learnable in a particular class.

## TL;DR

The first paper to frame Gröbner basis computation as a supervised learning problem: train a Transformer on (non-Gröbner set, Gröbner basis) pairs. The key technical contribution is an efficient backward dataset generation algorithm — starting from a Gröbner basis and transforming it to a non-Gröbner input via unimodular matrices — which is orders of magnitude faster than forward (Buchberger) generation. Experiments show Transformers achieve up to ~93% accuracy on 0-dimensional radical ideals over Q in 2 variables, with a measurable learnability gap between rational/real and finite-field coefficients.

## Problem

Computing a Gröbner basis from a polynomial system is NP-hard with doubly exponential worst-case complexity (Mayr & Meyer 1982; Dubé 1990). Despite heavy algorithmic investment (F4/F5, M4GB), large-scale instances remain intractable. Can a Transformer trained on (input, output) pairs learn to predict Gröbner bases without encoding any explicit algebraic procedure? And if so, how do you generate the millions of training pairs needed without running the expensive forward computation?

## Approach

**Dataset generation (Section 4):** The key insight is that 0-dimensional radical ideals are generically in *shape position* — their lex-reduced Gröbner basis has the form $G = \{h, x_1 - g_1, \ldots, x_{n-1} - g_{n-1}\}$ for univariate polynomials $h, g_1, \ldots, g_{n-1} \in k[x_n]$. This structure allows direct sampling of Gröbner bases (Problem 4.1). To construct non-Gröbner inputs with the same ideal (Problem 4.2, the "backward Gröbner problem"), the authors apply unimodular matrix transformations: given $G$, compute $F = U_1 P U_2 G$ where $U_1, U_2$ are unimodular upper-triangular matrices and $P$ is a permutation matrix. Theorem 4.6 guarantees $\langle F \rangle = \langle G \rangle$; Proposition 4.7 gives the sufficient condition for shape-position ideals with $s = n$.

**Architecture (Section 5–6):** Standard encoder-decoder Transformer (6 layers, 8 attention heads). Polynomials are tokenized in prefix notation with separator tokens. A *hybrid input embedding* handles coefficient tokens: discrete tokens for structural symbols; a small feed-forward network $f_E: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}^D$ for numeric coefficient values, avoiding vocabulary explosion for rational/real coefficients and implementing continuity bias (nearby numbers embed similarly).

**Experimental setup (Section 6):** 16 datasets $D_n(k)$ for $n \in \{2, 3, 4, 5\}$ and $k \in \{\mathbb{F}_7, \mathbb{F}_{31}, \mathbb{Q}, \mathbb{R}\}$. Training set: 1,000,000 samples; test set: 1,000 samples. Training time: less than one day on a single NVIDIA RTX A6000ada GPU. AdamW optimizer, batch size 16, 8 epochs.

## Key result

**Runtime of dataset generation (Table 1):** On 1,000 samples with $n$ variables over $\mathbb{Q}$, backward generation vs. forward (SageMath/libSingular):

| Method | $n=2$ | $n=3$ | $n=4$ | $n=5$ |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Forward (std) | 4.20 s | 216.3 s | 740.1 s† | 1411.1 s‡ |
| Forward (stdfglm) | 7.22 s | 8.29 s | 21.0 s | 164.3 s |
| **Backward (ours)** | **5.23 s** | **5.46 s** | **7.05 s** | **7.91 s** |

(† >13% timeouts; ‡ >25% timeouts at the 5-second per-instance limit.)

**Learnability (Table 2, accuracy % / support accuracy %)** on shape-position datasets $D^-_n(k)$:

| Field | $n=2$ | $n=3$ | $n=4$ | $n=5$ |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| $\mathbb{Q}$ (discrete emb.) | 93.7 / 95.4 | 88.7 / 92.0 | 90.8 / 94.0 | 86.5 / 90.6 |
| $\mathbb{Q}$ (hybrid emb.) | 66.8 / 87.3 | 69.0 / 89.8 | 62.7 / 86.8 | 0.0 / 84.9 |
| $\mathbb{F}_7$ (discrete emb.) | 72.3 / 79.1 | 78.1 / 83.2 | 71.3 / 84.6 | 84.3 / 88.5 |
| $\mathbb{F}_{31}$ (discrete emb.) | 46.8 / 77.3 | 50.2 / 80.9 | 51.1 / 83.7 | 28.6 / 77.9 |
| $\mathbb{R}$ (hybrid emb.) | 57.2 / 85.0 | 61.0 / 88.0 | 61.7 / 87.5 | 45.6 / 82.9 |

Over $\mathbb{Q}$ Cauchy module datasets: 99.7% / 99.8% at $n=2$ with discrete embedding.

**Key observation (Section 6.2):** Performance is higher on infinite fields ($\mathbb{Q}, \mathbb{R}$) than finite fields ($\mathbb{F}_p$), counterintuitively, because modular arithmetic lacks numeric continuity structure. Support accuracy (predicting the correct monomial support without necessarily predicting exact coefficients) is consistently higher than full accuracy. Incorrect predictions are "mostly reasonable" — often only a few coefficients are wrong while the support is correct; this suggests integration with Traverso's (1997) Hilbert-function-guided Buchberger algorithm as a hybrid.

## Assumptions

- Restricted to **0-dimensional radical ideals in shape position** (a generic but not universal class — covers most polynomial-system-with-solutions applications including post-quantum cryptography challenges).
- Training distribution determined by the backward generation procedure; model performance is in-distribution; no generalization claim to out-of-distribution ideal classes.
- Discrete embedding for $\mathbb{Q}$ coefficients restricts to $a, b \in \{-5, \ldots, 5\}$ for Gröbner basis and $\{-100, \ldots, 100\}$ for input polynomials.
- Standard Transformer architecture (vanilla encoder-decoder, 6 layers, 8 heads) — no architecture tuning.
- Term order fixed to $\prec_{\mathrm{lex}}$ (lexicographic); other term orders accessible via FGLM algorithm at $O(n \cdot \deg(h)^3)$ additional cost.

## Limitations / scope

- Not a replacement for Buchberger/F4/F5 on general polynomial systems — the approach is explicitly in-distribution. "Transformers cannot be an efficient tool for general Gröbner basis computation" (Section 4.1, verbatim).
- Hybrid embedding (regression for coefficients) underperforms discrete embedding for $\mathbb{Q}$; coefficient regression accumulates errors in autoregressive generation.
- No performance results for $n > 5$ variables.
- Finite-field performance is substantially weaker — modular arithmetic does not exhibit the numeric continuity that the embedding network exploits.
- Dataset density parameter $\sigma$ must be reduced as $n$ increases to keep sequences manageable (sequence length < 5,000); this limits the diversity of higher-variable training data.
- No runtime comparison between the Transformer's inference time and Buchberger at test time (though Appendix F.3 shows cases where the Transformer is faster).

## Why this paper matters

This paper opens a fifth strategy for Gröbner basis computation — learning — alongside the classical four (S-polynomial avoidance, efficient linear-algebraic reduction, modular computation, and structure-exploiting methods). Its primary technical contribution is not the Transformer architecture itself but the backward generation algorithm, which resolves a previously unaddressed algebraic problem. The backward Gröbner problem (Problem 4.2) and the random Gröbner basis generation (Problem 4.1) are novel contributions to computational algebra that arise from the ML framing and have independent algebraic interest.

The paper also contributes a practical hybrid embedding technique for handling numeric tokens in algebraic sequence-to-sequence tasks — a challenge shared by any system that processes polynomial expressions over infinite fields.

From the perspective of AI-for-symbolic-computation, this paper sits at the intersection of the "ML as solver heuristic" paradigm (cf. Peifer et al. 2020 for RL-guided S-polynomial selection in Buchberger, which is the closest prior work) and the "direct task learning" paradigm (cf. Lample & Charton 2020 for symbolic integration). The distinguishing feature is the doubly exponential NP-hard forward complexity — a harder barrier than symbolic integration or differential equations — and the algebraic novelty required to circumvent it via backward generation.

## Open questions surfaced

- Can the Transformer's predicted monomial support (high support accuracy even when full accuracy fails) be integrated with Traverso's (1997) Hilbert-function-guided Buchberger to create a hybrid algorithm? This was noted but not implemented.
- What is the learnability boundary as $n$ grows? The accuracy drop for $n = 5$ over $\mathbb{F}_{31}$ (28.6%) suggests a cliff; can architectural or data improvements sustain learnability to $n = 10+$?
- Can the backward generation method extend beyond 0-dimensional shape-position ideals to positive-dimensional ideals or non-radical ideals?
- Why does modular arithmetic resist learning? Is the lack of continuity structure fundamental, or can a different embedding resolve the finite-field learnability gap?
- Can the Transformer's inference time provide a consistent speedup over F4/F5 on in-distribution instances (App. F.3 shows some cases)? A systematic runtime comparison is absent.
- Does the backward Gröbner problem have applications in computational algebra outside of ML dataset generation (e.g., cryptographic protocol design, constructive proofs)?
