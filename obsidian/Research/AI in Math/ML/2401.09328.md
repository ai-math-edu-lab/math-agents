# Online Stability Improvement of Gröbner Basis Solvers using Deep Learning

## Abstract

Over the past decade, the Gröbner basis theory and automatic solver generation have lead to a large number of solutions to geometric vision problems. In practically all cases, the derived solvers apply a fixed elimination template to calculate the Gröbner basis and thereby identify the zero-dimensional variety of the original polynomial constraints. However, it is clear that different variable or monomial orderings lead to different elimination templates, and we show that they may present a large variability in accuracy for a certain instance of a problem. The present paper has two contributions. We first show that for a common class of problems in geometric vision, variable reordering simply translates into a permutation of the columns of the initial coefficient matrix, and that—as a result—one and the same elimination template can be reused in different ways, each one leading to potentially different accuracy. We then prove that the original set of coefficients may contain sufficient information to train a classifier for online selection of a good solver, most notably at the cost of only a small computational overhead. We demonstrate wide applicability at the hand of generic dense polynomial problem solvers, as well as a concrete solver from geometric vision.

## TL;DR

Xu et al. show that for a large class of polynomial systems used in geometric vision (permutation-invariant systems), different variable orderings produce the same elimination template up to column permutations of the coefficient matrix — each permutation yielding potentially different numerical stability. They train a small 4-layer neural network on synthetic data to predict, from the input coefficient matrix alone, which permutation will yield the most stable solution. The approach adds negligible runtime overhead (0.63–0.89 ms vs. 2.44–5.43 ms for brute-force enumeration) and demonstrably improves solver accuracy on benchmark geometric vision problems including camera pose estimation (UPnP).

## Problem

Automatically-generated Gröbner-basis solvers (via Kukelova et al.'s toolbox) choose a fixed elimination template offline. For a given problem instance, different variable orderings — corresponding to different column permutations of the coefficient matrix — can yield substantially different numerical stability. The question is: can the input coefficients alone tell us, at runtime (online), which permutation to use, without paying the cost of trying all n! possibilities?

## Approach

**Section 2 — Permutation-invariant polynomial systems:**

The paper identifies a class of polynomial systems (including dense polynomials and symmetric polynomials) where the monomial support is invariant under variable permutations. For such systems, all n! variable orderings produce the same elimination template structure; the only difference is a permutation of the columns of the initial coefficient matrix C. Specifically, applying permutation P to variables translates to replacing C by C' via:

c'ᵢ = c_{findindex(Pᵀαᵢ, o)}

where o is the set of exponent vectors (Section 2.4, eq. 9).

**Section 3 — Online classifier:**

A 4-layer fully-connected neural network takes the vectorized coefficient matrix vec(C) as input and outputs n! rank signals in [0,1] (one per permutation), trained with MSE loss against ground-truth permutation rankings derived by brute-force evaluation on synthetic data. Architecture: input layer (m×h inputs), three hidden layers (500 neurons each, ReLU activation, batch normalization), output layer (n! outputs, sigmoid activation). Training: Adam optimizer (lr=0.001, β₁=0.9, β₂=0.999, ε=10⁻⁸), batch size 128, 200 epochs. Permutation invariance is enforced via data augmentation — each training example generates n!−1 additional consistently-relabeled examples (Section 3.3, Figure 5).

**Section 4 — Evaluation:**

Tested on two generic dense polynomial problem solvers (3-variable/degree-3 and 4-variable/degree-2) and the UPnP camera resectioning algorithm (Kneip et al., 4 variables, degree 3, 55-dimensional input).

## Key result

**Timing (Section 4.1):** For the 3-variable problem, brute-force enumeration of n!=6 permutations requires 2.44ms; single evaluation with neural network prediction takes 0.63ms. For the 4-variable problem: 5.43ms vs. 0.89ms. Speed-up factor grows with n.

**Training data:** 100000 × n! samples per problem; 76000 × n! training, 12000 × n! validation, 12000 × n! test.

**UPnP result (Section 4.3):** The 55-dimensional classifier (4! = 24 permutations) achieves significant improvement in the error distribution for camera pose estimation, closely tracking the "oracle best permutation" distribution. The paper characterizes this as "quite important" because many geometric vision solvers parametrize rotation via quaternions or Cayley parameters, placing them in the permutation-invariant class and thus eligible for the same treatment.

**Conditions for benefit (Section 4.2):** Numerical stability only becomes predictable when coefficients cover a sufficiently large spectrum of magnitudes. Problems with uniform-magnitude coefficients showed no benefit. Problems with coefficients drawn from mixed ranges [0,1]/[0,10]/[0,100] showed increasing benefit as the range spread grew.

## Assumptions

- The polynomial system is permutation-invariant (monomial support unchanged under variable permutation) — this covers dense and degree-wise-dense polynomials, and many geometric-vision rotation parametrizations (quaternion, Cayley).
- The Gröbner basis solver and elimination template are fixed (generated offline by an automatic solver generator such as Kukelova et al.'s toolbox).
- The coefficient matrix C is available at runtime prior to calling the elimination template.
- Coefficient magnitudes must span sufficient dynamic range for the classifier to learn a meaningful signal (Section 4.2).
- Training is synthetic and noiseless — for geometric problems, this requires a forward simulation of the geometric scenario (Section 3.2).

## Limitations / scope

- The permutation-invariance property is necessary; the method does not apply to arbitrary polynomial systems where variable orderings produce genuinely different elimination templates.
- The paper only considers permutations as the perturbation class — it does not address choosing among fundamentally different elimination templates (though the authors note this as future work in Section 5).
- The geometric solver evaluation (UPnP) is noise-free; real-world robustness under measurement noise is not evaluated.
- The paper does not demonstrate that the learned classifier generalizes across problem types — a separate classifier must be trained per problem class.
- The symbolic computation community's interest (learning to compute or improve Gröbner bases) is orthogonal to this paper's contribution (learning to select among pre-computed solver instantiations).

## Why this paper matters

Xu et al. occupy a distinct niche in the ML-for-Gröbner literature. The Kera et al. and Peifer 2020 papers (batch siblings, not yet in vault) address the symbolic-computation question: can ML learn to compute Gröbner bases more efficiently, by learning S-polynomial selection or basis-computation strategies? Xu et al. sidestep that question entirely. Their Gröbner solver is a fixed black-box. What they learn is a meta-level selection policy: given a new problem instance, which instantiation of the solver (column permutation) will be numerically best?

This is an unusual application of deep learning to algebraic computation: the network never touches algebra. It operates on floating-point coefficients, learns to detect conditioning structure, and outputs a solver-selection decision. The result is practically significant for geometric vision pipelines, where Gröbner-basis solvers are used inside RANSAC loops and numerical stability directly affects the quality of structure-from-motion or camera calibration.

The cross-cutting insight — that variable ordering affects numerical conditioning of polynomial solvers in ways predictable from the coefficients — is generalizable beyond geometric vision to any domain using automatically-generated Gröbner-basis solvers. The method is also relevant to the ML-for-symbolic-computation discussion: it shows that a learned component can improve a symbolic algorithm without replacing the symbolic component.

## Open questions surfaced

- Can the permutation-selection approach be extended to cases where no single elimination template covers all permutations (i.e., genuinely different elimination templates exist for different orderings)? The authors flag this as future work (Section 5).
- Does the classifier generalize when real measurement noise is added (beyond the noise-free UPnP evaluation)?
- Can a single classifier learn across multiple permutation-invariant problem types (transferability), or must one be trained per problem class?
- For the symbolic-computation community: is there a way to combine this approach (online permutation selection) with methods that learn to compute Gröbner bases (Kera et al., Peifer 2020) to address both efficiency and numerical stability jointly?
- The paper proves the existence of sufficient information in the coefficients to train a classifier (Section 5) but does not characterize what structural property of the coefficients carries the signal. What feature of the coefficient spectrum predicts conditioning?
