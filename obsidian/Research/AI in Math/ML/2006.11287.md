# Discovering Symbolic Models from Deep Learning with Inductive Biases

## Abstract

"We develop a general approach to distill symbolic representations of a learned deep model by introducing strong inductive biases. We focus on Graph Neural Networks (GNNs). The technique works as follows: we first encourage sparse latent representations when we train a GNN in a supervised setting, then we apply symbolic regression to components of the learned model to extract explicit physical relations. We find the correct known equations, including force laws and Hamiltonians, can be extracted from the neural network. We then apply our method to a non-trivial cosmology example-a detailed dark matter simulation-and discover a new analytic formula which can predict the concentration of dark matter from the mass distribution of nearby cosmic structures. The symbolic expressions extracted from the GNN using our technique also generalized to out-of-distribution data better than the GNN itself. Our approach offers alternative directions for interpreting neural networks and discovering novel physical principles from the representations they learn."

## TL;DR

Train a Graph Neural Network with sparse latent representations; then apply symbolic regression to extract the symbolic equation the GNN has learned. Recovers known physics equations (force laws, Hamiltonians) and discovers a new cosmological formula for dark matter concentration. Foundational approach for using ML to find mathematical/physical equations.

## Problem

Deep learning models are accurate but not interpretable — you don't know *what equation* the model has learned. Can the implicit mathematical relationships encoded in a GNN's weights be explicitly extracted as symbolic equations that humans can read and verify?

## Approach

1. **GNN with sparse inductive bias**: train a GNN on the mathematical/physical dataset, encouraging latent representations to be sparse (few active components). This forces the GNN to find compact representations.
2. **Symbolic regression**: apply symbolic regression (e.g., genetic algorithm-based SR) to each component of the learned model separately. Because components are sparse, SR operates on simpler relationships than the full model.
3. **Validation**: check that extracted formulas match known physical laws (sanity check) and generalize to out-of-distribution data better than the original GNN.

The key insight: decomposing the problem (train GNN for accuracy, then extract symbolic form from components) sidesteps the difficulty of directly regressing a complex equation from raw data.

## Key result

- **Recovers known equations**: force laws, Hamiltonians, and other established physics equations are correctly extracted from GNNs trained on relevant data — validating the method.
- **Discovers new formula**: applying the method to dark matter simulations (N-body cosmological simulations) discovers a **new analytic formula** predicting dark matter concentration from neighboring mass distribution — not previously known to human researchers.
- **Better out-of-distribution generalization**: the symbolic formula extracted from the GNN generalizes better than the GNN itself to unseen data distributions.

## Assumptions

- GNNs with sparse latent representations capture the relevant mathematical structure of the domain.
- Symbolic regression can recover equations of reasonable complexity from the sparse components.
- The discovered formulas are mathematically exact (not just fits) — evidenced by out-of-distribution generalization.

## Limitations / scope

- Requires sparse-inductive-bias training upfront; not all GNN architectures are amenable.
- Symbolic regression scales poorly with formula complexity — works for equations of moderate length but not for very long formulas.
- Applied to physics/cosmology examples; applicability to pure mathematics (group theory, number theory) is untested.

## Replication evidence

PySR library (https://github.com/MilesCranmer/PySR) is publicly released and widely used. The dark matter formula has been cited and discussed in cosmology literature. The physics-equation recovery results are independently testable.

## Why this paper matters

Cranmer et al. 2020 establishes the **GNN + symbolic regression pipeline** as a practical tool for mathematical/physical discovery. The dark matter formula is a real scientific contribution: the system discovered something that human researchers hadn't previously written down.

This paper is foundational for the "ML discovers equations" paradigm, which includes:
- **Ramanujan Machine** ([[raayoni-2021-ramanujan]]): discovers conjectures about mathematical constants via exhaustive search + heuristics.
- **FunSearch** ([[romera-paredes-2023-funsearch]]): discovers new mathematical constructions via LLM-guided evolutionary search.

The progression: Cranmer 2020 extracts equations from learned neural network representations → Ramanujan Machine 2021 generates conjectures about mathematical constants → FunSearch 2023 discovers new combinatorial constructions. Each represents a distinct approach to ML-driven mathematical discovery.

**Cross-vault relevance**: the symbolic regression approach could be applied to B(2,5) word complexity data — fitting a symbolic formula to the empirical growth function $|P_s(2,5)|$ data from [[kuznetsov-shlepkin-2009]] and [[kuznetsov-shlepkin-2010]] might reveal whether there's a closed-form expression for the minimal-word growth rate.
