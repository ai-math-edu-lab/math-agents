# LeanDojo: Theorem Proving with Retrieval-Augmented Language Models

## Abstract

"Large language models (LLMs) have shown promise in proving formal theorems using proof assistants such as Lean. However, existing methods are difficult to reproduce or build on, due to private code, data, and large compute requirements. This has created substantial barriers to research on machine learning methods for theorem proving. This paper removes these barriers by introducing LeanDojo: an open-source Lean playground consisting of toolkits, data, models, and benchmarks. LeanDojo extracts data from Lean and enables interaction with the proof environment programmatically. It contains fine-grained annotations of premises in proofs, providing valuable data for premise selection: a key bottleneck in theorem proving. Using this data, we develop ReProver (Retrieval-Augmented Prover): an LLM-based prover augmented with retrieval for selecting premises from a vast math library. It is inexpensive and needs only one GPU week of training. Our retriever leverages LeanDojo's program analysis capability to identify accessible premises and hard negative examples, which makes retrieval much more effective. Furthermore, we construct a new benchmark consisting of 98,734 theorems and proofs extracted from Lean's math library. It features challenging data split requiring the prover to generalize to theorems relying on novel premises that are never used in training. We use this benchmark for training and evaluation, and experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of ReProver over non-retrieval baselines and GPT-4. We thus provide the first set of open-source LLM-based theorem provers without any proprietary datasets and release it under a permissive MIT license to facilitate further research."

## TL;DR

LeanDojo is an open-source infrastructure for ML-based theorem proving in Lean 4 — data extraction, programmatic interaction, benchmarks, and a trained model (ReProver). ReProver's key contribution: retrieval-augmented generation for premise selection — embed the proof goal and retrieve the most relevant lemmas from Mathlib 4, giving the LLM better context. Outperforms GPT-4 and non-retrieval baselines on a challenging new benchmark.

## Problem

**Two problems:**
1. **Research barriers**: existing LLM theorem provers use private code/data/compute, preventing community replication and building.
2. **Premise selection bottleneck**: Mathlib 4 has 100k+ lemmas; the prover must choose which ones to use in the current proof. Without retrieval, LLMs hallucinate or select irrelevant premises.

## Approach

**LeanDojo infrastructure:**
- **Data extraction**: programmatically interacts with Lean 4's elaborator to extract tactic proofs, premise annotations, and proof state data.
- **Benchmark**: 98,734 theorems extracted from Mathlib 4; split designed so test theorems require premises never seen during training (hard generalization test).
- **Programmable interface**: interact with Lean 4's proof environment from Python — check tactics, extract proof states, enumerate premises.

**ReProver (Retrieval-Augmented Prover):**
- **Embedding model**: trains a premise encoder that maps proof goals and premises to a shared embedding space.
- **Retrieval**: given the current proof goal, retrieve the top-k most similar premises from Mathlib 4's embedding index.
- **LLM prover**: feed the retrieved premises as context to an LLM that generates the next tactic.
- **Hard negatives**: use LeanDojo's program analysis to identify premises that are syntactically accessible but semantically irrelevant — training on these makes the retriever discriminative.

## Key result

- **ReProver outperforms non-retrieval baselines and GPT-4** on the new benchmark (specific pass rates in the paper body; abstract does not quote numbers).
- **98,734-theorem benchmark** with a "hard generalization split" — a more challenging and realistic evaluation than previous benchmarks.
- **1 GPU week of training** — dramatically more compute-efficient than alternative systems.
- **Open-source**: all code, data, and models released under MIT license.

## Assumptions

- Retrieval from a pre-built embedding index (Mathlib 4 premises) is effective even for novel proofs requiring premises outside the training distribution.
- The hard-negative training signal (accessible but irrelevant premises) generalizes to the test distribution.
- Mathlib 4 is a sufficiently diverse mathematical corpus to evaluate theorem proving capability.

## Limitations / scope

- Lean 4 / Mathlib 4 only; not applicable to Isabelle or Coq directly.
- ReProver is a tactic-level prover — it generates individual proof tactics, not full proof sketches.
- The benchmark's hard split tests generalization to new premises, but not to new mathematical domains beyond Mathlib 4's current scope.

## Replication evidence

Open-source: community has built on LeanDojo for subsequent research. [[2404.12534]] (Lean Copilot) is built on the LeanDojo infrastructure — the same author group extended it for human-AI interaction.

## Why this paper matters

LeanDojo makes three contributions:
1. **Open infrastructure**: removes private-code barriers; enables community research.
2. **Embeddings-for-math**: ReProver's premise encoder is the first production-quality embedding model for Lean 4 proof goals and premises — the "embeddings-for-math" paradigm instantiated at scale.
3. **Realistic benchmark**: the hard generalization split forces provers to work with unseen premises, unlike prior benchmarks that allowed test-premise memorization.

The premise selection problem is structurally similar to the KB rule injection problem in the Mixer: given the current KB state, which existing rules should be "imported" from another ordering's KB to help complete the current proof? LeanDojo's retrieval approach (embed the goal, retrieve relevant items) is a template for the Mixer's rule selection heuristic.

**Note on authorship**: Peiyang Song, Kaiyu Yang, and Anima Anandkumar appear in both LeanDojo ([[2306.15626]]) and Lean Copilot ([[2404.12534]]). LeanDojo is the research infrastructure; Lean Copilot is the interactive tool built on top. Both belong to the same research arc from the same group.
