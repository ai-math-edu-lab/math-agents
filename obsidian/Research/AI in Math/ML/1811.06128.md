# Machine Learning for Combinatorial Optimization: A Methodological Tour d'Horizon

## Abstract

"This paper surveys the recent attempts, both from the machine learning and operations research communities, at leveraging machine learning to solve combinatorial optimization problems. Given the hard nature of these problems, state-of-the-art algorithms rely on handcrafted heuristics for making decisions that are otherwise too expensive to compute or mathematically not well defined. Thus, machine learning looks like a natural candidate to make such decisions in a more principled and optimized way. We advocate for pushing further the integration of machine learning and combinatorial optimization and detail a methodology to do so. A main point of the paper is seeing generic optimization problems as data points and inquiring what is the relevant distribution of problems to use for learning on a given task."

## TL;DR

Surveys what ML has actually achieved for combinatorial optimization (NP-hard scheduling, routing, planning) vs. what is claimed. Frames the integration carefully: ML replaces handcrafted heuristics in decision procedures, not the overall search structure. Key methodological point: the problem distribution matters — a model trained on one instance distribution may fail completely on another. Provides the critical lens for evaluating AI-for-math claims (AlphaTensor, FunSearch, etc.).

## Problem

State-of-the-art combinatorial optimization algorithms use handcrafted heuristics (e.g., variable-ordering in SAT, branching decisions in branch-and-bound, restart strategies in local search). Can ML learn better heuristics? And if so, what has been empirically demonstrated vs. merely claimed?

## Approach

**Taxonomy of ML for CO integration:**
1. **End-to-end learning**: neural network directly predicts solutions (e.g., Pointer Networks for TSP). Pro: fast at inference. Con: rarely competitive with OR methods on hard instances; poor generalization.
2. **ML as a heuristic inside an exact solver**: replace one decision in an exact algorithm (e.g., variable selection in CDCL SAT) with a learned policy. Pro: retains exactness guarantee. Con: the learned policy must be competitive with handcrafted heuristics on the solver's own dynamics.
3. **Algorithm selection / portfolio**: ML predicts which algorithm from a portfolio will perform best on the current instance. Pro: exploits instance features to route to the right solver. Con: requires diverse portfolio and representative training set.

**Methodological critique:**
- "The relevant distribution of problems" is the crux: models trained on small random instances rarely transfer to structured large instances (the difference between academic benchmarks and industrial instances).
- Many claims of "ML beats OR" are on toy-size instances or easy distributions.
- True evaluation requires testing on the distributions that matter to practitioners.

## Key result

- **ML as heuristic works in specific niches**: learned branching policies in MIP (Mixed Integer Programming) can reduce solving time significantly — but only when training and test distributions match.
- **End-to-end learning is not competitive** with OR on hard instances; generalization is the core open challenge.
- **Algorithm selection is the most reliable application**: given a portfolio of good algorithms, ML for selection often outperforms individual algorithms.
- **Distribution mismatch** is the primary source of failure when ML for CO underperforms: the model has "learned the wrong problem."

## Assumptions

- The paper focuses on polynomial-size instances (typical for industrial CO); claims may not transfer to exponential-scale problems like Burnside group word search.
- The "distribution" framing assumes the problem instances have a well-defined distribution — appropriate for portfolio/industrial settings, less clear for mathematical structure discovery.

## Limitations / scope

- Survey up to ~2020; does not cover AlphaTensor (2022), FunSearch (2023), or AlphaEvolve (2025) — see [[RL/fawzi-2022-alphatensor]], [[ML/romera-paredes-2023-funsearch]], [[ML/2506.13131]] for those.
- Operations research focus (scheduling, routing, planning) — not specifically mathematical theorem proving or group theory.

## Replication evidence

Survey paper; reviews existing results. The methodological framework has been adopted by subsequent work as the standard evaluation checklist.

## Why this paper matters

Bengio et al. 2021 is the **calibrating lens** for all AI-for-math claims, including those in the existing AI in Math/ subtree. Key lessons for the Mixer:

1. **Portfolio ML is the most reliable**: choosing which ordering (RPO/shortlex/RPO-2) to use for the next injection step is an algorithm-selection problem — exactly the strongest ML-for-CO niche. A learned injection policy trained on a distribution of KB runs is a natural application.

2. **Distribution matters**: a Mixer injection policy trained on B(4,3) instances may not transfer to B(2,5) — the problem distributions are different. This is the core generalization challenge.

3. **ML as heuristic in exact search**: the Mixer is an exact procedure (if KB terminates, the result is provably correct). Using ML to select injection targets is the "ML as heuristic inside an exact solver" paradigm — the niche where Bengio et al. report genuine success.
