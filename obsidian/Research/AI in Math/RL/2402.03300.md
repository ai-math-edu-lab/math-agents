# DeepSeekMath: Pushing the Limits of Mathematical Reasoning in Open Language Models

## Abstract

"Mathematical reasoning poses a significant challenge for language models due to its complex and structured nature. In this paper, we introduce DeepSeekMath 7B, which continues pre-training DeepSeek-Coder-Base-v1.5 7B with 120B math-related tokens sourced from Common Crawl, together with natural language and code data. DeepSeekMath 7B has achieved an impressive score of 51.7% on the competition-level MATH benchmark without relying on external toolkits and voting techniques, approaching the performance level of Gemini-Ultra and GPT-4. Self-consistency over 64 samples from DeepSeekMath 7B achieves 60.9% on MATH."

## TL;DR

DeepSeekMath 7B: pretraining on 120B math tokens from Common Crawl + introducing GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimization), a PPO variant that removes the need for a separate critic model. 51.7% on MATH benchmark (approaching GPT-4) at only 7B parameters. GRPO became the standard RL algorithm for DeepSeek's subsequent math + formal reasoning work.

## Problem

Can open-source LLMs reach closed-model performance on mathematical reasoning benchmarks? And can RL be applied to math LLM fine-tuning more efficiently than PPO (which requires a separate critic model, doubling the compute)?

## Approach

**Two contributions:**

1. **Mathematical pretraining**: continues pretraining DeepSeek-Coder on 120B math-specific tokens curated from Common Crawl (web pages with math content, filtered and deduplicated). Includes natural language math text + code. This is the "DeepSeekMath" model.

2. **GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimization)**: a novel RL algorithm for fine-tuning. Instead of a separate value network (PPO's critic) to estimate advantage, GRPO estimates advantage from a group of generated samples for the same input — comparing within the group removes the need for a separate critic. Advantage = (reward of sample i − mean reward of group) / std of group. More compute-efficient than PPO; no reward model needed if a verifier provides exact rewards.

**RL fine-tuning**: applies GRPO using a combination of process reward models and outcome reward models as the reward signal.

## Key result

- **DeepSeekMath 7B**: 51.7% on MATH benchmark without external tools or voting — "approaching GPT-4" at 7B parameters.
- **With self-consistency (64 samples)**: 60.9% on MATH.
- **GRPO**: removes the separate critic model; training is ~2× more compute-efficient than PPO for the same RL training scenario.
- **Open model**: DeepSeekMath 7B released publicly.

## Assumptions

- 120B tokens from Common Crawl (with math filtering) captures sufficient mathematical knowledge for informal reasoning.
- GRPO's group-relative advantage estimate is a valid substitute for PPO's critic-based advantage — validated empirically in the paper.
- MATH benchmark score is a meaningful proxy for general mathematical reasoning capability.

## Limitations / scope

- Informal mathematical reasoning (chain-of-thought, GSM8K, MATH) — not formal theorem proving in Lean/Isabelle.
- 7B parameter model; larger models would achieve higher scores.
- GRPO's group-relative baseline can be noisy when the group has low diversity.

## Replication evidence

GRPO is reproduced and used in [[2504.21801]] (DeepSeek-Prover-V2, formal theorem proving context), confirming the algorithm's validity. The 51.7%/60.9% MATH numbers are independent of the GRPO algorithm per se — the community has replicated broadly.

## Why this paper matters

DeepSeekMath introduces **GRPO**, which becomes the standard RL fine-tuning algorithm for DeepSeek's subsequent AI-math systems (including [[2504.21801]] DeepSeek-Prover-V2). The "no separate critic" efficiency gain makes RL fine-tuning for math practical at large scale.

The pretraining insight (120B math tokens from Common Crawl) also mattered: it showed that web-scale math data, properly filtered, is sufficient to bootstrap competitive math reasoning without specialized datasets.

Both contributions — GRPO and large-scale math pretraining — directly informed DeepSeek-Prover-V2's architecture, making this paper the immediate predecessor of the 2025 formal theorem proving SOTA.
