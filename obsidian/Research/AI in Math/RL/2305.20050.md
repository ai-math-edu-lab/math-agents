# Let's Verify Step by Step

## Abstract

"In recent years, large language models have greatly improved in their ability to perform complex multi-step reasoning. However, even state-of-the-art models still regularly produce logical mistakes. To train more reliable models, we can turn either to outcome supervision, which provides feedback for a final result, or process supervision, which provides feedback for each intermediate reasoning step. Given the importance of training reliable models, and given the high cost of human feedback, it is important to carefully compare the both methods. Recent work has already begun this comparison, but many questions still remain. We conduct our own investigation, finding that process supervision significantly outperforms outcome supervision for training models to solve problems from the challenging MATH dataset. Our process-supervised model solves 78% of problems from a representative subset of the MATH test set. Additionally, we show that active learning significantly improves the efficacy of process supervision. To support related research, we also release PRM800K, the complete dataset of 800,000 step-level human feedback labels used to train our best reward model."

## TL;DR

Process reward models (PRMs) — reward each step of a mathematical reasoning chain — significantly outperform outcome reward models (ORMs — reward only the final answer) for training math LLMs. Process-supervised model solves 78% of MATH (challenging benchmark). Released PRM800K: 800k step-level human feedback labels. Foundational paper for the PRM paradigm.

## Problem

LLMs still make logical errors in multi-step math reasoning even when the final answer looks correct. Standard RLHF (outcome supervision) rewards the model only if the final answer is right — but this doesn't distinguish good reasoning with wrong answer from bad reasoning with lucky right answer. Can step-level supervision improve reliability?

## Approach

**Two supervision paradigms compared**:
- **Outcome supervision (ORM)**: reward model sees full solution and labels it correct/incorrect. Standard RLHF.
- **Process supervision (PRM)**: reward model evaluates each step of the solution independently. Annotators label each step as correct/incorrect.

**Active learning**: use the current PRM to select which steps to annotate next, focusing human effort on the most informative examples.

**PRM800K dataset**: 800,000 step-level human feedback labels collected via active learning. Released to enable external replication.

**Training**: fine-tune a large LM with RL using the PRM as the step-level reward signal.

## Key result

- **Process supervision: 78% on MATH** test subset vs. significantly lower for outcome supervision (exact ORM baseline number not quoted in the abstract; paper shows PRM substantially outperforms ORM).
- **Active learning** improves process supervision efficacy — focused annotation is more efficient than random.
- **PRM800K released**: 800k labeled steps from MATH problems; enables community follow-up.

## Assumptions

- Step-level correctness can be defined and annotated by humans consistently for mathematical reasoning.
- The step-level reward generalizes from training distribution (MATH dataset) to test problems.
- Active learning's annotation selection doesn't introduce systematic bias.

## Limitations / scope

- Human annotation: expensive, subjective at ambiguous steps, doesn't scale automatically.
- MATH dataset scope: competition math (AMC, AIME, etc.) — may not generalize to research-level.
- The paper proposes PRMs as a training signal but doesn't fully automate the annotation (later papers like [[2312.08935]] address this).

## Replication evidence

[[2312.08935]] (Math-Shepherd) directly replicates and extends this: achieves similar performance using *automatically* generated step-level labels (no human annotation), validating the core PRM insight. The PRM800K dataset enables community replication.

## Why this paper matters

Lightman et al. 2023 establishes the key insight: **step-level feedback is better than outcome feedback** for mathematical reasoning training. This has become a universal assumption in all subsequent math RL systems:
1. [[2312.08935]] (Math-Shepherd): auto-generates PRM training data without humans.
2. [[2402.03300]] (DeepSeekMath): uses GRPO with process-level rewards.
3. [[2504.21801]] (DeepSeek-Prover-V2): uses step-level subgoal reward from Lean 4 verification — every subgoal is a "step" with a formal correctness signal.

The deep connection: formal proof verification in Lean is a *perfect* PRM — every tactic application gives an exact, automatic, non-ambiguous process reward (accepted by Lean 4 or not). Lightman's insight is that you need step-level rewards; Lean 4 provides them for free. [[alphaproof-2024]] is the culmination of this idea.

**Cross-vault connection**: the PRM paradigm is relevant to the Mixer's rule injection strategy. Each rule injection event is a "step" in the KB completion process; a reward model that evaluates injection quality at each step (did the injection accelerate convergence?) is a natural PRM for the Mixer domain.

- Can step-level labels be automatically generated (without human annotation) at scale? [[2312.08935]] partially answers yes.
- Does process supervision generalize from MATH to research-level mathematics?
- What is the right granularity for "a step" in complex mathematical reasoning?
