# Math-Shepherd: Verify and Reinforce LLMs Step-by-step without Human Annotations

## Abstract

"In this paper, we present an innovative process-oriented math process reward model called Math-Shepherd, which assigns a reward score to each step of math problem solutions. The training of Math-Shepherd is achieved using automatically constructed process-wise supervision data, breaking the bottleneck of heavy reliance on manual annotation in existing work. We explore the effectiveness of Math-Shepherd in two scenarios: 1) Verification: Math-Shepherd is utilized for reranking multiple outputs generated by Large Language Models (LLMs); 2) Reinforcement Learning: Math-Shepherd is employed to reinforce LLMs with step-by-step Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO). With Math-Shepherd, a series of open-source LLMs demonstrates exceptional performance. For instance, the step-by-step PPO with Math-Shepherd significantly improves the accuracy of Mistral-7B (77.9%→84.1% on GSM8K and 28.6%→33.0% on MATH)."

## TL;DR

Automatic PRM training without human annotation: train a step-level verifier using automatically labeled process supervision (derived from whether a solution step is eventually completed correctly). Two applications: verification (reranking LLM outputs) and RL (step-level PPO). 77.9%→84.1% on GSM8K for Mistral-7B. Removes the human-annotation bottleneck of Lightman's PRM800K.

## Problem

[[2305.20050]] (Lightman et al.) showed PRMs outperform ORMs, but required 800,000 human-labeled steps — expensive and not scalable. Can process-level supervision be constructed automatically, without human annotation?

## Approach

**Automatic PRM data generation**: for each training problem, generate multiple solution attempts using the LLM. For each step in the solution, determine whether any continuation from that step eventually leads to a correct final answer. Label the step as correct if such a continuation exists (verified by checking the final answer). This is a **completability label** — the step is rewarded if the solution *can be completed* correctly from there.

**Two use cases:**
1. **Verification (best-of-N)**: use Math-Shepherd as a verifier to rerank multiple solution candidates — select the one with the highest average step score.
2. **RL (step-level PPO)**: use Math-Shepherd rewards to train via step-level PPO, reinforcing steps that lead to correct completions.

## Key result

- **Mistral-7B + step-level PPO**: 77.9% → **84.1%** on GSM8K; 28.6% → **33.0%** on MATH.
- **With verification (reranking)**: further boosts to 89.1% (GSM8K) and 43.5% (MATH).
- **No human annotation**: the process-wise labels are generated automatically — the entire pipeline runs without human involvement.

## Assumptions

- The completability label (step is correct if a correct completion exists) is a valid proxy for step-level correctness.
- The LLM can generate a sufficient diversity of completions to detect completable vs. dead-end steps.
- Results on GSM8K/MATH generalize beyond these benchmarks.

## Limitations / scope

- The completability label conflates "this step is correct" with "this step doesn't block a correct solution" — a subtle distinction for proofs with non-unique paths.
- Informal math (GSM8K, MATH) only; not formal theorem proving in Lean.
- Requires many LLM rollouts per problem (computationally intensive for complex problems).

## Replication evidence

[[2402.03300]] (DeepSeekMath) incorporates Math-Shepherd's approach as part of GRPO training — implicit replication by the same group. ACL peer review provides external validation.

## Why this paper matters

Math-Shepherd breaks the human-annotation bottleneck: the most expensive part of [[2305.20050]]'s PRM was collecting 800k human labels. Math-Shepherd shows that **automatic completability labeling** achieves similar training signal — making PRM training scalable.

This matters for any domain with an automatic verifier:
- **Formal math**: Lean 4 provides perfect automatic step-level labels (tactic accepted/rejected). [[alphaproof-2024]] and [[2504.21801]] are natural extensions — they use Lean 4 as the Math-Shepherd for formal proof.
- **KB completion (Mixer)**: each rule injection attempt could be labeled by whether it eventually accelerates KB convergence — a Math-Shepherd-style automatic PRM for the Mixer domain.

## Open questions surfaced

- Is the completability label as accurate as human step-level annotation? (Systematic evaluation not in this paper.)
- Can Math-Shepherd be adapted to formal theorem proving (Lean 4), where each step has an exact binary correctness signal?
