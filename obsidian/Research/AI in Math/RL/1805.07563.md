# Reinforcement Learning of Theorem Proving

## Abstract

"We introduce a theorem proving algorithm that uses practically no domain heuristics for guiding its connection-style proof search. Instead, it runs many Monte-Carlo simulations guided by reinforcement learning from previous proof attempts. We produce several versions of the prover, parameterized by different learning and guiding algorithms. The strongest version of the system is trained on a large corpus of mathematical problems and evaluated on previously unseen problems. The trained system solves within the same number of inferences over 40% more problems than a baseline prover, which is an unusually high improvement in this hard AI domain. To our knowledge this is the first time reinforcement learning has been convincingly applied to solving general mathematical problems on a large scale."

## TL;DR

First large-scale RL application to automated theorem proving: MCTS simulations guided by RL-learned policies and value estimates, trained on a large HOL Light corpus. 40% more problems solved than the baseline prover within the same inference budget. The foundational RL-for-ATP paper.

## Problem

Standard ATP provers use handcrafted heuristics to guide connection-style proof search. Can RL replace these heuristics, learning a value function and policy from experience with previous proofs?

## Approach

**Connection-style proof search**: the prover explores proof states using connection calculus (a form of goal-directed proof). No domain heuristics — search is guided entirely by a learned policy and value function.

**RL training**: runs Monte-Carlo simulations (MCTS) from previous proof attempts. Successful and failed attempts both provide training signal. The RL loop: attempt proofs → observe outcomes → update policy/value network → attempt harder proofs.

**Multiple versions**: different combinations of learning algorithms (neural networks, gradient boosted trees) and guiding mechanisms evaluated. Strongest version is neural-network-guided.

**Scale**: trained on a large corpus of HOL Light mathematical problems; evaluated on a held-out test set.

## Key result

- **40% more problems solved** within the same number of inferences as the baseline prover.
- "An unusually high improvement in this hard AI domain" — the authors note this is larger than typical improvements in ATP competitions.
- **First large-scale RL for general mathematical problems**: prior RL in games (AlphaGo etc.) required well-defined game-state structure; this adapts the paradigm to the less-structured theorem proving setting.

## Assumptions

- Connection-style proof search is a suitable framework; other proof calculi (tableaux, superposition) are not evaluated.
- HOL Light theorems are representative of the broader mathematical theorem proving challenge.
- MCTS exploration is sufficient to generate useful training signal without hand-crafted problem decomposition.

## Limitations / scope

- HOL Light only; not demonstrated on Lean, Isabelle, or Metamath.
- The setting differs from LLM-based provers: the policy/value networks take structured proof states as input, not natural language.
- Performance measured as "problems solved within N inferences" — may not translate directly to wall-clock time.

## Replication evidence

The paradigm is replicated and extended in [[2205.11491]] (HyperTree), which applies MCTS + RL to Lean-based proof search with significantly stronger language models. The core architecture is confirmed sound.

## Why this paper matters

Kaliszyk et al. 2018 establishes RL as viable for theorem proving without domain heuristics. Two subsequent developments build directly on this:
1. **HyperTree** ([[2205.11491]]) imports MCTS + online RL into the Lean/transformer-LLM setting — the same core idea with much stronger models.
2. **AlphaProof** ([[alphaproof-2024]]) uses AlphaZero-style RL with Lean 4 and Gemini — a direct descendant.

The "no domain heuristics" framing is important: it means the RL signal alone (proof success/failure) is sufficient to learn good proof strategies, without expert knowledge engineering.

**Cross-vault connection**: the connection between RL for theorem proving and the Mixer is structural. The Mixer uses feedback from KB convergence/cascade to drive rule selection — this is a form of RL signal (the cascade trajectory rewards good rule injection choices). Kaliszyk's paper is the precedent for using search outcome feedback to learn search strategies.
