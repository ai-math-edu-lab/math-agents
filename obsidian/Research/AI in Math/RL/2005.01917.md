# Learning Selection Strategies in Buchberger's Algorithm

> **Translation note**: vault language is English. Source paper is in English; no translation required.

## Abstract

Studying the set of exact solutions of a system of polynomial equations largely depends on a single iterative algorithm, known as Buchberger's algorithm. Optimized versions of this algorithm are crucial for many computer algebra systems (e.g., Mathematica, Maple, Sage). We introduce a new approach to Buchberger's algorithm that uses reinforcement learning agents to perform S-pair selection, a key step in the algorithm. We then study how the difficulty of the problem depends on the choices of domain and distribution of polynomials, about which little is known. Finally, we train a policy model using proximal policy optimization (PPO) to learn S-pair selection strategies for random systems of binomial equations. In certain domains, the trained model outperforms state-of-the-art selection heuristics in total number of polynomial additions performed, which provides a proof-of-concept that recent developments in machine learning have the potential to improve performance of algorithms in symbolic computation.

## TL;DR

Peifer, Stillman, and Halpern-Leistner frame S-pair selection in Buchberger's algorithm as an MDP and train a PPO policy network on random binomial ideals. On the benchmark distributions they test (n=3, d=20, s=4 or s=10), the learned agent uses 22–37% fewer polynomial additions than the best classical heuristics (Degree/Normal). This is the first paper to apply RL directly to the inner pair-selection loop of Buchberger's algorithm, opening a new ML-for-symbolic-computation research direction and providing a concrete, reproducible benchmark environment.

## Problem

Buchberger's algorithm for computing a Gröbner basis contains a free choice at each iteration: which S-pair to process next (the "select" step in Algorithm 2). This choice does not affect correctness — the algorithm terminates regardless — but profoundly affects computational cost. All existing selection strategies (Degree, Normal, Sugar, First) are human-designed heuristics. The paper asks: can an RL agent learn a better selection strategy automatically, and what is a suitable distribution over polynomial ideals for training and benchmarking?

## Approach

**MDP formulation** (Section 3): At each time step t, state s_t = (G_t, P_t) consists of the current generating set and current pair set. The action set A_t = P_t (choose one pair). Reward r_t = −1 × (number of polynomial additions in the S-polynomial reduction of the selected pair). Trajectory ends when P is empty (algorithm terminates with a Gröbner basis). Objective: maximize expected return E[∑γ^{t−1} r_t] with γ=1, i.e., minimize total polynomial additions.

**Domain: random binomial ideals** (Section 3.1): To make the state representable by a fixed-architecture network, restrict to polynomials with at most two terms (binomials). All intermediate generators produced by Buchberger's algorithm on a binomial input are also binomials, so the state representation stays valid throughout. Two distributions: "weighted" (lower average degree) and "uniform" (higher average degree). Coefficient field: Z/32003Z throughout.

**Network architecture** (Section 4.1): State (G, P) encoded as a p×4n matrix (one row per pair, concatenating the exponent vectors of both polynomials in the pair). A two-layer 1D convolutional network (4n → 128 → 1, using 1×1 kernels to process all pairs simultaneously) maps each row to a preference score; softmax gives the policy. Observation excludes coefficients — partially observed environment.

**Value function for variance reduction** (Section 4.2): GAE (Generalized Advantage Estimation) with λ=0.97, γ=0.99. The baseline value V(G,P) = number of polynomial additions required to complete Buchberger's algorithm from state (G,P) using the Degree strategy — computed exactly by a full rollout, expensive but effective.

**Training** (Section 4.3): PPO with ε=0.2, Adam optimizer, learning rate 0.0001, max 80 gradient updates per epoch, early-stopping when sampled KL-divergence exceeds 0.01. 100 episodes per epoch, 2500 epochs. Training data generated online (no fixed dataset). Training wall time: 16–48 hours on AWS c5n.xlarge instances.

## Key result

**Primary result** (Table 3, Section 5): Agents trained on n=3, d=20 distributions and evaluated on 10,000 new random ideals:

| s | Distribution | Degree (best baseline) | Agent | Improvement vs. Degree |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 10 | Weighted | 136. [50.9] | 85.6 [27.3] | 37% [stddev reduced 46%] |
| 4 | Weighted | 160. [64.5] | 101. [44.9] | 37% [stddev reduced 30%] |
| 10 | Uniform | 197. [55.7] | 141. [42.8] | 28% [stddev reduced 23%] |
| 4 | Uniform | 195. [70.0] | 151. [56.4] | 22% [stddev reduced 19%] |

(Numbers are mean [stddev] of polynomial additions. Smaller is better. — Section 5 / Table 3.)

**Five-variable experiments** (Section 5.4): "In the direction of increasing n, we trained and tested our model (with the same hyperparameters) on binomial ideals in five variables. The agents use on average 48% fewer polynomial additions than the best benchmark strategy in the 5-10-10 weighted distribution, 28% fewer in 5-5-10 weighted, and 11% fewer in the 5-5-10 uniform distribution."

**Strategy interpretation** (Section 5.1): The agent exhibits three identifiable behaviors: (a) mimicking Degree selection (43.5% probability of selecting a pair Degree would choose), (b) preferring pairs whose S-polynomial is a monomial (31.7% rate when such pairs exist), (c) preferring pairs whose S-polynomial is low degree (48.3% rate of selecting minimal-S-polynomial-degree pair). Hard-coding behaviors (b) and (c) yields "TrueDegree" — 12% improvement over Degree — and a monomial-first variant — 1.2% improvement. Neither matches the agent's full 37% gain.

**Complexity bounds** (Appendix C): Upper bound: degmax(GB(I)) ≤ 2(d²/2+d)^{2n−1} (Dubé 1990, double exponential). Lower bound: "there exists an ideal J_n, generated by quadratic homogeneous pure binomials in 2^{2n}−1 variables such that for any monomial order, 2^{2n−1}−1 ≤ degmax(GB(J_n))" (Koh 1998, Theorem 3 / Appendix C). For generic random inputs: degmax(GB(I)) ≤ (n+1)(d−1)+1 (almost surely, outside a measure-zero set).

## Assumptions

- Restricted to **binomial ideals** over a finite field Z/32003Z (avoids coefficient blowup, keeps state representation fixed-size).
- Restricted to **n ≤ 5 variables** and **degree d ≤ 30** in primary experiments (Section 3.1 / 5.4).
- Pair elimination uses standard Gebauer–Möller rules (1988) throughout — these are not learned.
- The value function uses a full Degree-strategy rollout at every step (Section 4.2) — expensive; the paper notes this squares the computational complexity and is a bottleneck for scaling.
- Performance metric is **polynomial additions** (implementation-independent), not wall-clock time.

## Limitations / scope

- Results on **non-binomial polynomials are mixed**: on sparse non-binomial ideals (binomial + Poisson(λ) extra monomials), the agent is bimodal — greatly better than benchmarks on many ideals, but random on others. The derived TrueDegree strategy achieves 19–33% improvement over Degree for all λ tested (Table 6), but the neural agent in mean underperforms Degree at λ≥0.3.
- **No real-time performance testing**: the paper explicitly defers testing wall-clock improvement "to future work" (Section 4.1). The implementation-independent metric (polynomial additions) may not translate to practical speedup.
- **Generalization is approximate**: agents trained on weighted perform slightly worse on uniform and vice versa (Table 5), and performance degrades as distribution parameters move away from the training setting (Figure 6).
- The expensive exact value function (full Degree rollout) is a scaling bottleneck; the paper flags "a more efficient modeled value function is crucial for scaling" (Section 5.4).

## Replication evidence

The paper includes three independent training runs per configuration (Appendix A) and reports best smoothed performance. ## Why this paper matters

This paper establishes the first RL benchmark for the pair-selection step of Buchberger's algorithm — the core computational bottleneck in Gröbner basis computation. By framing the algorithm as an MDP and demonstrating that a simple PPO policy beats classical heuristics by 22–37%, it opens a concrete ML-for-symbolic-computation research direction that several subsequent groups have extended (notably the Kera et al. papers on learning to compute Gröbner bases directly).

The choice of binomial ideals as the training domain is a technically motivated simplification: it keeps the state space tractable, is directly relevant to integer programming and cryptography applications, and includes the worst-known doubly-exponential complexity examples. The empirical study of how difficulty varies across distributions (Section 3.2, Tables 1–2) is itself a contribution — this statistical characterization of Buchberger's algorithm's behavior had not been published before.

The interpretability analysis (Section 5.1) is notable: the learned strategy is partly explained by two novel heuristics (prefer pairs whose S-polynomial is a monomial; prefer pairs whose S-polynomial is minimal degree) that the authors hard-code as TrueDegree. This is an example of RL-guided heuristic discovery: the RL agent finds a strategy, inspection of the strategy suggests an interpretable rule, and that rule outperforms prior art when extracted and applied directly.

The paper's limitations are clearly stated. The Cornell group (with Stillman as a core Macaulay2 developer) is in an unusual position to evaluate whether 22–37% fewer additions would translate to practical speedup in production CAS implementations — and they explicitly decline to make that claim without further work.

## Open questions surfaced

- **Scaling to larger n**: preliminary 5-variable results are promising (up to 48% improvement) but the expensive value function prevents a thorough search. Can a learned or approximated value function replace the full Degree rollout? (Section 5.4)
- **Non-binomial generalization**: the bimodal performance on sparse polynomials suggests the agent has not learned a general strategy. Can a network with access to coefficients or richer features generalize? (Section 5.4)
- **Real-time speedup**: do 22–37% fewer polynomial additions translate to measurable wall-clock improvement in Macaulay2, Singular, or Sage? The paper defers this entirely. (Section 4.1)
- **Other algorithm choices**: "select" is one of three free choices in Algorithm 2 (alongside "reduce" and "update"). Can RL be applied to the other two?
- **Connection to F4/F5 algorithms**: Faugère's F4 and F5 algorithms (cited) use signature-based selection rather than pair selection. Can RL enhance those too, or is the MDP formulation specific to the Buchberger loop?
- **Does the agent's strategy generalize across monomial orders?** All experiments use grevlex; it is unknown whether a grevlex-trained agent transfers to other monomial orders.
