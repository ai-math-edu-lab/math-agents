# DeepSeek-Prover-V2: Advancing Formal Mathematical Reasoning via Reinforcement Learning for Subgoal Decomposition

## Abstract

"We introduce DeepSeek-Prover-V2, an open-source large language model designed for formal theorem proving in Lean 4, with initialization data collected through a recursive theorem proving pipeline powered by DeepSeek-V3. The cold-start training procedure begins by prompting DeepSeek-V3 to decompose complex problems into a series of subgoals. The proofs of resolved subgoals are synthesized into a chain-of-thought process, combined with DeepSeek-V3's step-by-step reasoning, to create an initial cold start for reinforcement learning. This process enables us to integrate both informal and formal mathematical reasoning into a unified model. The resulting model, DeepSeek-Prover-V2-671B, achieves state-of-the-art performance in neural theorem proving, reaching 88.9% pass ratio on the MiniF2F-test and solving 49 out of 658 problems from PutnamBench. In addition to standard benchmarks, we introduce ProverBench, a collection of 325 formalized problems, to enrich our evaluation, including 15 selected problems from the recent AIME competitions (years 24-25). Further evaluation on these 15 AIME problems shows that the model successfully solves 6 of them. In comparison, DeepSeek-V3 solves 8 of these problems using majority voting, highlighting that the gap between formal and informal mathematical reasoning in large language models is substantially narrowing."

## TL;DR

2025 SOTA formal theorem prover in Lean 4: uses DeepSeek-V3 to decompose complex theorems into subgoal chains recursively, synthesizes those chains into CoT training data, then trains via RL. Open weights, 671B model. 88.9% on MiniF2F-test — the 2025 ceiling for benchmark theorem proving.

## Problem

Prior LLM provers (GPT-f, HyperTree) struggle with long proofs requiring multi-step reasoning. Can recursive subgoal decomposition — using a strong informal reasoner (DeepSeek-V3) to plan the proof structure, then training a formal prover with RL on those structures — close the gap between informal and formal mathematical reasoning?

## Approach

**Recursive decomposition pipeline** (cold-start data generation):
1. Prompt **DeepSeek-V3** (a strong informal math LLM) to decompose a target theorem into subgoals.
2. Attempt to prove each subgoal with a formal Lean 4 prover.
3. Successful subgoal proofs are assembled into a **chain-of-thought proof trace** (interleaved informal reasoning + formal Lean steps).
4. These traces become the **cold-start training data** for RL.

**Reinforcement learning** (from cold start):
- Train the 671B model via RL where the reward is formal proof correctness (Lean 4 verification).
- The model learns to generate both CoT reasoning (informal) and Lean 4 proof steps (formal) in a unified output.

**Two generation modes** at inference:
- **Non-CoT mode**: direct Lean proof (fast, low-token).
- **CoT mode**: interleaved informal reasoning + formal steps (slower, higher accuracy on hard problems).

**ProverBench**: new evaluation dataset of 325 problems including 15 formalized AIME 24-25 problems. Addresses benchmark saturation on miniF2F.

## Key result

- **MiniF2F-test**: **88.9%** pass ratio — 2025 SOTA, vs. prior best ~65-70%.
- **PutnamBench**: 49/658 problems solved (Putnam is graduate-level competition math; prior SOTA was far lower).
- **AIME 24-25 (15 problems)**: 6/15 solved formally vs. 8/15 by DeepSeek-V3 informally — "the gap between formal and informal mathematical reasoning is substantially narrowing."
- **Open weights**: released, enabling community replication.

## Assumptions

- DeepSeek-V3's informal decomposition provides valid proof structure even when it can't formally close sub-lemmas.
- Recursive decomposition terminates for sufficiently many problems (not all theorems decompose cleanly).
- The 671B model scale is necessary for SOTA — smaller scale versions exist but with lower accuracy.

## Limitations / scope

- 88.9% on MiniF2F is high but not 100%; 11% of MiniF2F-test problems remain unsolved even at 671B scale.
- Putnam (49/658 ≈ 7.5%) shows current limits on research-level math.
- AIME gap (6/15 formal vs. 8/15 informal) shows formal proofs are still harder than informal reasoning.
- Leans 4 only; not applied to Isabelle or Coq.

## Replication evidence

Open weights released — community replication underway as of submission date. The benchmark numbers are from the paper itself; independent evaluation pending.

## Why this paper matters

DeepSeek-Prover-V2 is the 2025 quantitative milestone for formal theorem proving:
1. **88.9% MiniF2F** is the number the field will cite as the 2025 state of the art.
2. **Recursive subgoal decomposition** is a new training paradigm: instead of learning from existing library proofs (GPT-f) or from search traces (HyperTree), it generates its own structured training curriculum by decomposing hard problems into tractable subgoals. This is more scalable.
3. **Informal-formal unification**: the model produces both reasoning chains and formal Lean code — bridging Wu et al. (2205.12615) autoformalization and HyperTree (2205.11491) proof search in a single model.
4. **Open weights**: unlike AlphaProof (closed), DeepSeek-Prover-V2 enables community research.

**Cross-vault connection**: the subgoal decomposition paradigm is directly analogous to the Mixer's rule injection strategy — decompose B(2,5) into tractable subproblems (quotient group quotients), solve each, assemble. The formal analogy: DeepSeek-Prover-V2's training pipeline on group-theory Lean 4 problems (Mathlib has substantial group theory) could potentially be used to find or verify B(2,5) structural results.
