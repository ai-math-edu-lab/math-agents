# Lean Copilot: Large Language Models as Copilots for Theorem Proving in Lean

## Abstract

"Neural theorem proving combines large language models (LLMs) with proof assistants such as Lean, where the correctness of formal proofs can be rigorously verified, leaving no room for hallucination. With existing neural theorem provers pretrained on a fixed collection of data and offering valuable suggestions at times, it is challenging for them to continually prove novel theorems in a fully autonomous mode, where human insights may be critical. In this paper, we explore LLMs as copilots that assist humans in proving theorems. We introduce Lean Copilot, a general framework for running LLM inference natively in Lean."

## TL;DR

Lean Copilot integrates LLMs as real-time tactic suggesters within the Lean 4 IDE — humans write proofs, LLMs suggest next steps, fill goals, and search premises. Not fully autonomous. 74.2% automation rate, 85% better than the built-in aesop tactic. The human-AI collaboration paradigm for formal math, distinct from autonomous provers.

## Problem

Fully autonomous provers (GPT-f, HyperTree) work well on benchmark problems but struggle with genuinely novel theorems requiring human insight. Rather than replacing human mathematicians, can LLMs *assist* them interactively within the Lean 4 IDE workflow?

## Approach

**Lean Copilot framework**: a general infrastructure for running LLM inference natively within Lean 4 (no context-switching to external tools). Three primary LLM-powered capabilities:
1. **Tactic suggestion**: given current proof state, suggest likely next tactics (any pretrained model or cloud API can plug in).
2. **Proof goal completion**: attempt to close a specific goal with a generated proof term.
3. **Premise selection**: suggest relevant lemmas from Mathlib 4 given the current context.

**Human-in-the-loop**: the human accepts, rejects, or modifies suggestions. The LLM proposes; the Lean checker verifies; the human decides. This is fundamentally different from autonomous provers.

**Model agnostic**: plug in any LLM (local or cloud) — the framework handles the Lean 4 interface. Default models: fine-tuned models from the LeanDojo family.

## Key result

Evaluated on the **Mathematics in Lean** textbook:
- **Automation rate**: 74.2% of proof steps automated (vs. 40.1% for `aesop` — the best built-in Lean tactic automation).
- **Manual steps reduced**: Lean Copilot requires 2.08 manually-entered proof steps per theorem vs. 3.86 for `aesop` — a 46% reduction in human effort.
- **85% better than `aesop`** in automation rate.

## Assumptions

- The human mathematician remains in the loop; LLM suggestions are verified by Lean before acceptance.
- The Lean 4 / Mathlib 4 ecosystem is the target; the framework is designed for this ecosystem specifically.
- Task difficulty is bounded to textbook-level proofs (Mathematics in Lean); harder research proofs may require more human effort.

## Limitations / scope

- Not a standalone autonomous prover; requires a human to drive the proof.
- Evaluated on textbook-level proofs (Mathematics in Lean), not competition or research-level.
- Tactic suggestion quality depends on the underlying LLM; default LeanDojo models are not as powerful as GPT-4 or Gemini for hard problems.

## Replication evidence

Open source at https://github.com/lean-dojo/LeanCopilot. Community use reported; no formal independent replication cited in this note.

## Why this paper matters

Lean Copilot opens a different product category from autonomous provers: human-AI collaboration on formal proofs. This is important because:
1. **Real-world use**: most mathematicians using Lean 4 want assistance, not full automation. Lean Copilot addresses the actual workflow.
2. **Novel theorems**: autonomous provers struggle with genuinely novel theorems (not in any training distribution); human insight + LLM automation handles these.
3. **Lean 4 ecosystem integration**: by running LLM inference natively in Lean 4, Lean Copilot removes the context-switching overhead that plagues external LLM calls.

**Cross-vault connection**: if the B(2,5) finiteness result is ever formalized in Lean 4, Lean Copilot is the natural tooling for the formalization effort. The Mixer team could use Lean Copilot to guide the formal proof of B(4,3) finiteness (already established computationally) from the `algo-mixing-burnside-slides` results.
