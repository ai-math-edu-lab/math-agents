# HyperTree Proof Search for Neural Theorem Proving

## Abstract

"We propose an online training procedure for a transformer-based automated theorem prover. Our approach leverages a new search algorithm, HyperTree Proof Search (HTPS), inspired by the recent success of AlphaZero. Our model learns from previous proof searches through online training, allowing it to generalize to domains far from the training distribution. We report detailed ablations of our pipeline's main components by studying performance on three environments of increasing complexity. In particular, we show that with HTPS alone, a model trained on annotated proofs manages to prove 65.4% of a held-out set of Metamath theorems, significantly outperforming the previous state of the art of 56.5% by GPT-f. Online training on these unproved theorems increases accuracy to 82.6%. With a similar computational budget, we improve the state of the art on the Lean-based miniF2F-curriculum dataset from 31% to 42% proving accuracy."

## TL;DR

Replaces GPT-f's linear beam search with AlphaZero-style MCTS over the proof tree (HTPS), combined with online training on previously-failed proof attempts. Delivers large gains over GPT-f: 56.5% → 82.6% on Metamath, 31% → 42% on miniF2F. The foundational agentic tree-search prover architecture.

## Problem

GPT-f and its successors use beam search over proof steps — but this is shallow: it doesn't plan multiple steps ahead or backtrack effectively. Can AlphaZero's MCTS paradigm, adapted to proof trees, give a principled search that plans further ahead and learns from failures?

## Approach

**HTPS algorithm**: treats the proof tree as a search tree, where nodes are proof states and edges are tactic applications. Uses MCTS to balance exploration vs. exploitation, with a learned value function estimating provability of each state. Unlike standard MCTS (finite horizon games), proof trees can have infinite branching — hence "HyperTree" (handles DAG structures where states can be reached multiple times).

**Online training**: after each proof search (successful or not), trains the policy + value network on the observed states and outcomes. This differs from GPT-f's offline expert iteration: the model continuously updates from live search trajectories.

**Environments**: three environments of increasing difficulty (Metamath, lean-gym Lean 3, miniF2F).

## Key result

- **Metamath**: GPT-f baseline 56.5% → HTPS 65.4% (offline) → HTPS + online training **82.6%**
- **miniF2F-curriculum (Lean)**: prior SOTA 31% → HTPS **42%**
- **Ablation finding**: online training is the largest single contributor; the MCTS component adds another significant increment.

## Assumptions

- Proof search can be framed as a tree search with a learnable value function.
- Online training on failed search traces provides a useful learning signal.
- Generalizes to "domains far from the training distribution" — the online component is critical for this.

## Limitations / scope

- Infinite branching factor in proof trees requires approximations; performance degrades on very long proofs.
- Online training requires live interaction with a proof assistant (not purely offline).
- miniF2F results (42%) are substantial but still far from human expert performance.

## Replication evidence

The MCTS-for-proof-search paradigm has been adopted by [[2504.21801]] (DeepSeek-Prover-V2), AlphaProof ([[alphaproof-2024]]), and other systems. The basic architecture is considered sound and widely replicated.

## Why this paper matters

HyperTree establishes two principles that define the 2022–2025 state of the art:
1. **Tree search over proof trees** (not just linear generation) is essential for long proofs — beam search runs out of depth.
2. **Online learning from failed proofs** is more data-efficient than offline expert iteration — the model learns from what it couldn't prove, not just from what it could.

Both of these feed directly into [[alphaproof-2024]] (AlphaProof uses RL from proof search outcomes, essentially HTPS with scaled compute) and [[2504.21801]] (DeepSeek-Prover-V2 uses RL from subgoal-level proof outcomes).
