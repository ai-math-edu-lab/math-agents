# Draft, Sketch, and Prove: Guiding Formal Theorem Provers with Informal Proofs

## Abstract

Converts informal proofs into formal proof sketches that guide automated theorem provers toward simpler sub-problems. Tests scenarios with both human-written and language-model-generated informal proofs. Large language models successfully generate structured formal sketches reflecting the same logical progression as their informal counterparts, improving automated prover performance from approximately 21% to 39% success rates on mathematical competition problems.

## TL;DR

The key insight: don't translate informal proofs directly to formal proofs (Wu et al. 2022 — low success rate), but instead use them to generate a *sketch* that decomposes the formal proof into tractable sub-lemmas. 21% → 39% on competition math. Foundational autoformalization scaffolding paper.

## Problem

Autoformalization (Wu et al. [[2205.12615]]) has a 25% success rate on direct translation — the rest fail because formal proofs require strict rigor that informal proofs skip. Can informal proofs be used as **structural guides** (not as directly translated formal proofs) to decompose a formal proof problem into simpler sub-problems that automated provers can handle?

## Approach

Three-stage pipeline (giving the paper its name):
1. **Draft**: an LLM generates an informal proof of the theorem from scratch (or uses a human-written informal proof).
2. **Sketch**: the informal proof is translated to a formal "sketch" — a Isabelle/HOL proof skeleton with `sorry`-holes at the hard steps, expressing the logical structure without all details. The LLM also does this translation.
3. **Prove**: a formal theorem prover (Sledgehammer, a tactics-based Isabelle solver) fills in the `sorry`-holes. Each hole is now a smaller subproblem with a clearer goal.

Results: tested with (a) human-written informal proofs as the draft and (b) LLM-generated informal proofs as the draft. Both improve over baseline formal provers.

## Key result

- Baseline (no informal guidance): ~21% of competition problems formally proved.
- **DSP with LLM-generated informal proofs**: ~39% success rate.
- The sketch decomposition is more valuable than direct autoformalization: the sketch's logical structure guides the prover even when individual formal steps require repair.
- With human-written informal proofs as drafts: further improvement (human proofs have better logical structure than LLM-generated ones, but LLM-generated are sufficient to achieve the main gains).

## Assumptions

- Informal proofs carry useful structural information (logical flow, key lemmas) that can guide formal provers even if the details are wrong.
- The sketch language (Isabelle `sorry`-stubs) is expressive enough to capture proof structure.
- Sledgehammer (automated Isabelle prover) can fill in the leaf goals given correct decomposition.

## Limitations / scope

- Isabelle/HOL only; not demonstrated on Lean 4.
- LLM-generated informal proofs are noisy; improvement is large but not to human-level.
- The sketch step may fail if the LLM's informal proof has wrong logical structure (missing key lemma, incorrect ordering).
- Long proofs with many sub-lemmas may overwhelm the Sledgehammer sub-prover.

## Replication evidence

The informal-proof-guided approach has been adopted in follow-up work (the approach is considered sound). No systematic independent replication within this vault.

## Why this paper matters

DSP resolves the central tension in autoformalization: direct translation fails (Wu et al.: 25.3%), but informal proofs contain exactly the human insight that makes hard problems tractable. The answer is structural decomposition: use the informal proof's logical skeleton (the sketch) to create smaller formal problems, rather than trying to translate the proof verbatim.

This paper is the ancestor of the informal-to-formal pipeline used in virtually all modern scaled ATP systems. In particular:
- [[alphaproof-2024]] uses a version of this: Gemini generates informal approaches, AlphaProof searches for formal Lean proofs guided by the informal insight.
- [[2504.21801]] uses recursive subgoal decomposition (a structured formalization of DSP's sketch idea) as its core training signal.

Cross-vault relevance: DSP is directly applicable to Burnside group proofs. If informal proofs of B(2,5) finiteness or word equality become available (e.g., if the Mixer finds a computational proof), DSP-style autoformalization could convert them to Lean 4 formal proofs with the sketch decomposition approach.

## Open questions surfaced

- Can the sketch approach handle research-level mathematics where informal proofs are more complex and the prover sub-lemmas are harder?
- How does performance change when targeting Lean 4 instead of Isabelle? (Lean 4 is more expressive but Sledgehammer has no direct equivalent.)
