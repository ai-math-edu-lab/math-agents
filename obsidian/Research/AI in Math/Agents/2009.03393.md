# Generative Language Modeling for Automated Theorem Proving

## Abstract

"We explore the application of transformer-based language models to automated theorem proving. This work is motivated by the possibility that a major limitation of automated theorem provers compared to humans -- the generation of original mathematical terms -- might be addressable via generation from language models. We present an automated prover and proof assistant, GPT-f, for the Metamath formalization language, and analyze its performance. GPT-f found new short proofs that were accepted into the main Metamath library, which is to our knowledge, the first time a deep-learning based system has contributed proofs that were adopted by a formal mathematics community."

## TL;DR

GPT-f applies a transformer language model to formal theorem proving in Metamath (and preliminary experiments in Lean): sample candidate proof steps from the LLM conditioned on the goal state, verify correctness formally. Simple paradigm — sample-then-verify — that all subsequent LLM provers refine. Landmark: first LLM proof adopted into the Metamath library.

## Problem

Can transformer language models generate useful proof steps for formal theorem provers? Prior automated theorem provers relied on handcrafted heuristics or tabular search; the conjecture is that LLMs' ability to generate natural mathematical text can translate to formal proof step generation.

## Approach

Frames proof search as conditional language generation: given current proof state (goal + hypotheses in Metamath syntax), sample next proof step from a GPT-2/GPT-3 style transformer trained on a large Metamath proof corpus. Correct steps (verified by Metamath checker) are used for training via expert iteration: generate, verify, filter, retrain. Applies beam search and sampling at inference. Also presents preliminary results on Lean.

## Key result

- **Metamath**: GPT-f achieves 56.22% on a held-out test set, compared to 0% for pure tactic search without a learned model. More importantly, GPT-f found **new short proofs** accepted into the main Metamath library — the first time a deep-learning system contributed formally verified proofs to an active math library.
- **Lean (preliminary)**: proof success rates reported but not competitive with domain-specialized systems at the time.
- **Expert iteration**: training on the model's own successful proofs improves performance iteratively.

## Assumptions

- Formal math corpora (Metamath, Lean) are sufficient training data for an LLM to learn proof syntax and strategy.
- The sample-then-verify paradigm is sound: any proof the verifier accepts is correct by construction.
- Performance scales with model size and training data volume (consistent with general LLM scaling).

## Limitations / scope

- Works on Metamath (a specialized but less expressive formalism); Lean results are preliminary.
- No planning beyond beam search at the top level — proof search is shallow (single-step generation).
- No handling of long-horizon proofs requiring novel auxiliary lemmas.

## Replication evidence

The core paradigm (LLM → sample → verify → filter → retrain) has been replicated and extended in [[2205.11491]] (HyperTree), [[2210.12283]] (DSP), [[2504.21801]] (DeepSeek-Prover-V2), and dozens of other systems. The paradigm is considered correct and general.

## Why this paper matters

GPT-f is the founding paper of the LLM-for-formal-theorem-proving paradigm. Every subsequent paper in this batch (HyperTree, DSP, DeepSeek-Prover-V2, Lean Copilot) builds on or explicitly compares against GPT-f's architecture. The key contributions are:
1. **Sample-then-verify**: the simplest possible integration of LLMs with formal proof systems, and still competitive.
2. **Expert iteration**: using the model's own correct proofs as self-generated training data — a technique that later becomes the basis for RL-based training in AlphaProof and DeepSeek-Prover-V2.
3. **Practical adoption**: having proofs accepted into Metamath's library demonstrated that LLM-generated proofs can be novel and useful, not just reproductions.

## Open questions surfaced

- Can LLMs generate genuinely novel auxiliary constructions (not just recombinations of library tactics)?
- Does GPT-f's performance scale predictably with model size, and if so, at what scale does it become competitive with expert mathematicians?
- How does performance compare on Lean 4 / Mathlib 4 (the current frontier), where proofs are significantly more complex?
