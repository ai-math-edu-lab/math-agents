# Autoformalization with Large Language Models

## Abstract

"Autoformalization is the process of automatically translating from natural language mathematics to formal specifications and proofs. A successful autoformalization system could advance the fields of formal verification, program synthesis, and artificial intelligence. While the long-term goal of autoformalization seemed elusive for a long time, we show large language models provide new prospects towards this goal. We make the surprising observation that LLMs can correctly translate a significant portion (25.3%) of mathematical competition problems perfectly to formal specifications in Isabelle/HOL. We demonstrate the usefulness of this process by improving a previously introduced neural theorem prover via training on these autoformalized theorems. Our methodology results in a new state-of-the-art result on the MiniF2F theorem proving benchmark, improving the proof rate from 29.6% to 35.2%."

## TL;DR

First systematic demonstration that LLMs can directly translate informal math (AMC/AIME competition problems) to formal Isabelle/HOL specifications — 25.3% perfect translation rate, "surprisingly" high. Uses the autoformalized theorems to augment a theorem prover's training data, pushing MiniF2F 29.6% → 35.2%. Foundational paper for informal-to-formal translation as a technique.

## Problem

Formal verification requires problems stated in formal languages (Lean, Isabelle, Coq), but most mathematics is written informally. Can LLMs bridge this gap automatically, without human formalization labor? And can the generated formal statements improve theorem provers even if some are imperfect?

## Approach

1. Takes a pre-trained LLM (GPT-f style) and prompts it to translate natural-language AMC/AIME competition problems to Isabelle/HOL specifications. No fine-tuning specifically for autoformalization — the LLM generalizes from its pretraining.
2. Filters translated statements for syntactic validity (Isabelle typechecks them).
3. Attempts to prove the filtered statements using a neural theorem prover.
4. Successfully proved statements are used as additional training data for the prover.

## Key result

- **Translation accuracy**: 25.3% of AMC/AIME problems perfectly translated to Isabelle/HOL specifications on first try.
- **Prover improvement**: training on autoformalized theorems pushes MiniF2F proof rate from **29.6% → 35.2%** — a meaningful improvement with no additional human labeling.
- **Observation**: the 25.3% rate is described by the authors as "surprising" — the field had considered the task much harder before this result.

## Assumptions

- LLMs have sufficient exposure to formal mathematics (Isabelle, Lean) in pretraining to generalize to translation without explicit task-specific training.
- Syntactic validity (type-checking) is a sufficient filter; semantically correct but syntactically valid incorrect translations are treated as noise.
- AMC/AIME competition problems are representative of the informal-to-formal translation challenge.

## Limitations / scope

- 25.3% success rate = 74.7% failure rate; the system cannot reliably formalize difficult problems.
- Isabelle/HOL is the target; generalization to Lean 4 or Coq is not demonstrated.
- Semantic correctness of translation is not fully verified (some type-valid formulations may not faithfully capture the original problem).

## Replication evidence

[[2210.12283]] (Draft-Sketch-Prove) directly extends this approach, achieving better results by decomposing the translation into a sketch + formal completion step rather than direct translation.

## Why this paper matters

This paper established that LLMs have latent autoformalization capability — not just proof generation capability — without task-specific training. The 25.3% number became a standard benchmark. More importantly, it showed the **data flywheel**: autoformalization generates new training problems for provers, which improves prover performance, which could generate more formalization targets. This flywheel is now central to all scaled ATP systems (AlphaProof trains on millions of auto-generated problems; DeepSeek-Prover-V2 uses a recursive decomposition pipeline to generate subgoal training data — both are descendants of this core idea).

Cross-vault relevance: any future formalization of Burnside group theory results in Lean 4 (e.g., formalizing the B(4,3) computation from `algo-mixing-burnside-slides`) would benefit from autoformalization infrastructure. Wu et al. is the foundational reference for that pipeline.

## Open questions surfaced

- Can modern LLMs (GPT-4 level) achieve >50% autoformalization accuracy on AMC/AIME-level problems?
- Does the data flywheel (autoformalize → prove → retrain) continue to produce gains at scale, or does it saturate?
- Can autoformalization be applied to research-level mathematics (not just competition problems)?
