# Normal Subgroup

## Definition

A subgroup N ≤ G is **normal** in G (written N ⊴ G) if for all g ∈ G:
$$gNg^{-1} = N$$

Equivalently: N is closed under conjugation by all elements of G. Equivalently: gN = Ng for all g ∈ G (left cosets equal right cosets).

*Source: D&F §3.1, Definition.*

## Why it matters

Normal subgroups are precisely the kernels of group homomorphisms. They are the building blocks of the quotient construction: G/N is a well-defined group iff N ⊴ G. Without normality, the set of cosets does not form a group under coset multiplication.

The key structure theorem: every group G sits in a short exact sequence
$$1 → N → G → G/N → 1$$
for any N ⊴ G. Understanding G becomes: understand N, understand G/N, and understand how they fit together (extension problem).

## Examples

- **Alternating group Aₙ ⊴ Sₙ:** index 2, so automatically normal.
- **Center Z(G) ⊴ G:** gzg⁻¹ = z for all z ∈ Z(G) by definition.
- **Commutator subgroup [G,G] ⊴ G:** generated by all commutators [a,b] = a⁻¹b⁻¹ab.
- **Every subgroup of an abelian group:** gNg⁻¹ = N trivially since ab = ba.

## Simple groups

A group G is **simple** if it has no normal subgroups except {e} and G itself. Simple groups are the "atoms" of group theory — they cannot be factored further by the quotient construction. Examples: ℤ/pℤ (prime p), alternating groups Aₙ (n ≥ 5), the 26 sporadic simple groups.

## Normal closure

The **normal closure** of a set S in G is the smallest normal subgroup of G containing S:
$$⟪S⟫ = \bigcap \{N ⊴ G \mid S \subseteq N\} = \langle gsg^{-1} \mid g \in G, s \in S \rangle$$

This is the key construction in finitely presented group theory: G = F(X) / ⟪R⟫ where ⟪R⟫ is the normal closure of the relator set R in F(X).
