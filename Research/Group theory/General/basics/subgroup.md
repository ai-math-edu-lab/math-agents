# Subgroup

## Definition

Let G be a group. A subset H ⊆ G is a **subgroup** of G (written H ≤ G) if H forms a group under the same binary operation as G. Equivalently, H is a subgroup iff:

1. H is nonempty (equivalently, e ∈ H).
2. H is closed under the operation: for all a, b ∈ H, a · b ∈ H.
3. H is closed under inverses: for all a ∈ H, a⁻¹ ∈ H.

**Subgroup criterion:** H ≤ G iff H is nonempty and for all a, b ∈ H, ab⁻¹ ∈ H. (D&F §1.1, Proposition 2.)

*Source: D&F §1.1.*

## Why it matters

Subgroups are how we decompose a group into smaller, more tractable pieces. Lagrange's theorem — |H| divides |G| for finite G — is the foundational structural result, and it follows directly from the geometry of cosets of H in G. Subgroups also appear as kernels of homomorphisms (always normal) and as stabilizers of group actions.

## Examples

- **ℤ ≤ (ℚ, +):** integers form a subgroup of rationals.
- **{e, (1 2)} ≤ S₃:** the transposition generates a subgroup of order 2 in S₃.
- **Center Z(G) = {z ∈ G | zg = gz for all g ∈ G}:** always a normal abelian subgroup of G.
- **⟨g⟩ = {gⁿ | n ∈ ℤ}:** the cyclic subgroup generated by a single element g.

## Lagrange's theorem

If G is finite and H ≤ G, then |H| divides |G|. The index [G:H] = |G|/|H| equals the number of left (or right) cosets of H in G.
